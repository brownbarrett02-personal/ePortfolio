My Story

My Music Journey

Music has been a constant thread running through my life long before engineering became my focus. I started playing piano at age 7; my grandparents signed me up for lessons after hearing me pick out familiar tunes on their old piano.

Through my years with the Youth Orchestras of San Antonio, I had the opportunity to accompany world-class soloists, perform Beethoven Symphonies, and perform Gershwin in Carnegie Hall. Playing in an ensemble taught me things I couldn't learn anywhere else: how to listen as carefully as you play, how to subordinate individual ego to a collective sound, and how patience and precision compound over time.

When I arrived at Texas A&M, continuing to play was never a question. Joining the Chamber Orchestra has been one of the most grounding parts of my college experience, a weekly reminder that engineering isn't the only discipline that rewards rigor and craft. There's something about sight-reading a difficult passage and watching it come together over weeks of rehearsal that maps almost perfectly onto the iterative problem-solving I do in classes and my research.

Music isn't a hobby I compartmentalize from the rest of my life. It's a lens. The teamwork and leadership skills that I have developed in orchestral settings have translated directly into my leadership activities and research. Seriously studying music has allowed me to develop in ways that most people don't get to: making connections between unrelated things, demanding rigor and discipline at every turn, and allowing me to develop a fantastic memory that has aided me in every collegiate course I have taken.

Youth Orchestras of San Antonio
Played Cello with YOSA from 2018 to 2022: ensembles include "Sinfonietta Strings", "Repertory Strings", "YOSA Symphony" (as principal) and "YOSA Philharmonic".
