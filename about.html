Background

Who I Am

Howdy! I'm Barrett Brown — a junior at Texas A&M University from San Antonio, Texas, where I graduated as Valedictorian from MacArthur High School in 2022.

I came to Texas A&M with an unusual combination of passions: a deep love of engineering and physical science, and an equally deep belief that good policy is what determines whether technical innovation actually helps people. That tension is what drives everything I do here.

My double major in Aerospace Engineering and Political Science isn't a hedge — it's a commitment. I want to understand aerospace systems from the inside, work as a practicing engineer, and then spend the rest of my career ensuring that the institutions governing science and technology are worthy of the discipline itself.

Outside of academics, I've been heavily involved in Honors leadership as a Junior Advisor for LAUNCH, Texas A&M Student Government as an elected Senator, Class Councils, Fish Camp, and the Chamber Orchestra. I believe that a full life — one that includes service, community, and art — makes a better engineer.

I'm a National Merit Scholar and a recipient of the President's Endowed Scholarship, and I've been fortunate to intern at Southwest Research Institute and Bray International, R&D environments where I got to do real engineering work early in my college career.

Vision

Where I'm Headed

Short Term
Practice Engineering
Before moving into policy, I want real engineering experience — running simulations, designing hardware, working on systems that fail in real and interesting ways. That credibility is the foundation everything else is built on.
Long Term
Science & Engineering Policy
My goal is to work as an advocate for better science and engineering policy — shaping how institutions govern technology, fund research, and make decisions that affect engineers and the public alike.
Philosophy
Depth Before Breadth
Good policy advocacy requires genuine expertise. I'm not interested in opining on technical fields I don't understand — so I'm spending my formative years going deep before I go broad.
Guiding Belief
Engineers Should Be in the Room
Too many policy decisions about science and technology are made without engineers at the table. I want to change that — by being the kind of engineer who can also speak the language of governance.
